Functional principle of our tension spring

Gas tension springs work on the opposite principle to gas pressure springs , i.e. the gas pressure in the cylinder pulls the piston rod inwards. When a flap is closed, the hand force is supported by the tension spring. A gas tension spring is a self-contained and maintenance-free element. It consists of a pressure tube and a piston rod with a piston. There is also a seal on the piston rod guide to prevent possible gas loss. The gas tension spring is filled with compressed gas - nitrogen - which is used to provide the spring force. When the piston rod is not under load, the piston rod is always retracted. When the piston rod is pulled out, the volume in the cylinder decreases and the gas is compressed. This results in an increase in force (progression) of the gas spring. This depends on the diameter of the piston rod and the volume of the cylinder. The piston has a small opening (nozzle) through which the gas can flow, enabling a defined extension speed of the piston rod. The cross-section (diameter) of this opening determines the degree of damping of a spring.

Areas of application

Gas tension springs are used wherever a normal gas pressure spring cannot be used due to space constraints. They support the manual force when opening flaps that have to be pulled upwards. This type of installation can be found, for example, in elevator exit hatches. Gas tension springs are also used when flaps need to be held open, as is the case with desks with a fold-out work surface, for example.
